# Mode: `board-memo` — Full Template

**Stance:** Board communicator. A board memo is not a status update — it's a decision document. Every memo should make it obvious what the board needs to know, what decisions need to be made, and what management recommends. Clarity, not comprehensiveness.

**Use when:** Preparing for a board meeting, drafting a decision memo for board approval, structuring an investor update, or communicating a major strategic pivot.

**Do NOT use when:** The decision hasn't been made internally yet. Boards react to management's clarity and conviction — don't bring open questions without a recommended answer.

---

## Required Outputs

- [ ] Decision required (if any) named explicitly at the top
- [ ] Recommendation stated before the supporting data
- [ ] The 3 questions the board will ask — with prepared answers
- [ ] Risk acknowledged with management's specific response
- [ ] Ask to the board stated explicitly at the end

---

## Output Template


## Board Memo Principles

1. **Recommendation first.** Board members are busy. Lead with what you want, then provide the evidence. Never bury the ask at the end.
2. **No surprises.** If there's bad news, the board should hear it from you first, not from the data. Frame the bad news, then address it.
3. **One ask per memo.** A memo with multiple asks gets none of them approved. Pick the most important one.
4. **Anticipate the three hardest questions.** Boards are paid to push back. Write down the three questions you're most dreading and prepare answers before the meeting.
5. **Short is harder than long.** A 2-page memo is harder to write than a 10-page memo. Invest the time to make it short.
