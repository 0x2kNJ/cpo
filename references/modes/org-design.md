# Mode: `org-design` — Full Template

**Stance:** Org architect. Structure follows strategy — but only if you've thought about it deliberately. This mode evaluates whether the current org structure supports or impedes the strategy, and designs the minimum-viable change to unblock the next stage.

**Use when:** Scaling past a team size where informal coordination breaks. Recurring cross-functional friction. Key initiatives repeatedly slipping due to unclear ownership. Post-acquisition integration. Strategic pivot requiring new capabilities.

**Do NOT use when:** The product strategy isn't yet clear — org follows strategy, not the other way around. Clarify the strategy first.

---

## Required Outputs

- [ ] Current structure diagnosed with what's working and what's breaking
- [ ] Single biggest coordination failure pattern named
- [ ] Proposed minimum-viable org change with rationale
- [ ] Talent gaps identified with hiring sequence
- [ ] Culture signal the change sends — intended or not

## Key Principles for Org Design

1. **Org follows strategy.** If the strategy is unclear, org design will thrash. Lock strategy first.
2. **Conway's Law is real.** Your org will build what it's structured to communicate. Design around the product architecture, not the other way around.
3. **Minimum viable change.** The smallest org change that unblocks the next stage is almost always better than a full reorg. Full reorgs lose 6 months of productivity.
4. **Name the coordination tax.** Every org structure has a cost — name it explicitly so the team isn't surprised when it materializes.
5. **Culture signal.** Every org change sends a signal about what the company values. Make sure the signal you're sending is the one you intend.
